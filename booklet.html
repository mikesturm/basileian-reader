THE EALOI CANON

A Translation from the Original Languages

Preliminary Working Draft

Translated from the Westcott-Hort Greek text (NT material),

the Kirsopp Lake Greek text (Didache),

the Sahidic Coptic of Nag Hammadi Codex II,2 (Thomas),

and assorted critical editions for apocryphal fragments.

Preface

This is a working translation of the Jesus Tradition canon — a working set of source-language texts judged reliable as testimony to what Jesus said and did during his life and earthly presence (including resurrection, appearances, and ascension). It supersedes the earlier EALOI (Earliest Accessible Layer of Interpretation) canon. Where the EALOI selection used parsimony as its filter — the least-mediated source — this canon uses reliability for Jesus material specifically. Theological reflection about Jesus drops out; testimony to Jesus's words and deeds is what remains, even where the source is heavily mediated otherwise.

The translation prioritizes literal fidelity to source-language structure, lexical range, and historical-cultural context over conventional theological vocabulary. Strangeness in the Greek is allowed to produce strangeness in the English where the alternative would be domestication. Theologically loaded or polysemic terms are transliterated on first occurrence per pericope, with a proposed English rendering in brackets and a substantive endnote on first occurrence in the document.

Divine names are preserved in their original-language distinctions: Christos is rendered "Anointed," not "Christ"; anarthrous Kyrios in LXX citation is rendered "YHWH"; Kyrios as christological title for Jesus is rendered "Lord"; Kyrios as ordinary address is rendered contextually ("sir," "master"). Huios tou anthrōpou is rendered "the son of the human being." Theos is rendered "God" (anarthrous) or "the God" (with article, where Greek emphasis is present). Pater is rendered "Father." Where Jesus uses Abba (Mark 14:36), the Aramaic transliteration is preserved with the Greek translation following.

Translator's notes are placed as endnotes at the end of the document and numbered consecutively. A companion volume of working notes contains the standing glossary, decision log, and convention log; standing decisions documented there are applied throughout this translation.

The text-base for the canonical NT material is the Westcott-Hort 1881 critical edition; WH differs from the modern NA28/UBS5 in approximately 558 places (Aland's count), and significant divergences are flagged in the endnotes. The longer ending of Mark (16:9–20) is excluded; the original gospel ends at 16:8. For the Q material, both the Lukan and Matthean witnesses are translated, since Q is reconstructed and no manuscript exists; the translation generally treats the Lukan version as the primary witness (per Kloppenborg's methodology) and notes Matthew's parallel where it diverges substantively. For the Johannine material, only the pericopes treated as historical-narrative witness are included; the Johannine theological discourses, the Logos prologue, and the egō eimi discourses are excluded as Johannine theological composition rather than dominical speech. For the Pauline first-person appearance claims, the visionary-encounter caveat (per Galatians 1:16, en emoi) is preserved structurally — the form is given, but the category-difference from the synoptic appearance narratives is named in the endnotes. For the apocryphal material in Tier 3, each fragment is given a brief introductory note describing its date, source, and the scholarly judgment that brings it into the canon.

Tier 1: Primary Witnesses

These sources are reliable for Jesus material across their content (with redactional awareness for the gospels) and constitute the bulk of the canon.

kata Markon, c. 65–70 CE. The earliest narrative gospel. Translation ends at 16:8 with the original gospel; the so-called "longer ending" (16:9–20) is excluded as a 2nd-century addition.

E. The Gospel of Thomas (ⲡⲉⲩⲁⲅⲅⲉⲗⲓⲟⲛ ⲡⲕⲁⲧⲁ ⲑⲱⲙⲁⲥ) — Selected Sayings

Sahidic Coptic, Nag Hammadi Codex II,2, c. 350 CE manuscript (translation of an originally Greek collection, likely 1st-century in its earliest layer). Greek fragments preserved in P.Oxy 1, 654, and 655 are cited where they parallel the Coptic. Standard editions: Layton 1989 (Nag Hammadi Codex II,2–7, NHS XX); Plisch 2008.

The included sayings are those satisfying the corroboration criterion: a logion is included as a unit (whole-logion principle) when independent attestation in early Christian or Jewish streams supports any portion of it, and when no clear borrowing from non-Christian or non-Jewish ascetic-philosophical traditions overwhelms the corroborative material.

Coptic-specific conventions: nomina sacra such as ⲓ︤ⲥ︥ (Iēsous) and ⲡ̄ⲛ︤ⲁ︥ (pneuma) are preserved as "Jesus" and pneuma respectively; bracketed lacunae [ ] mark restored or damaged text; angle-brackets ⟨ ⟩ mark editorial supplements; the Coptic ⲧⲙⲛ̄ⲧⲉⲣⲟ (kingdom) is rendered "basileia — reign" maintaining consistency with the Greek convention; ⲡⲓⲱⲧ (Father) and ⲡⲏⲩⲉ (heavens) are rendered straightforwardly. The Greek loanword monachos is preserved untranslated with bracketed gloss "single-ones / solitary-ones" on first occurrence; this term is theologically loaded in Thomas (it appears in Logia 16, 49, 75) and any single English rendering loses semantic range. Eikōn (Genesis 1:26–27 vocabulary) is preserved untranslated with bracketed gloss in Logion 22.61

Excluded from this canon's selection of Thomas (with reasons):

- Logia 1, 4, 10, 12, 13, 15, 18, 19, 23, 25, 27, 28, 30, 32–34, 37, 38, 42, 43, 49–53, 56, 58–62, 67, 69–71, 77, 80–85, 87, 88, 93, 97, 98, 102, 105, 106, 108, 110–112, 114 — distinctively esoteric, body-suspicious, or proto-gnostic sayings (e.g., 50: origins-from-light cosmology with Valentinian "movement and rest"; 56 and 80: world-as-corpse / body-as-corpse encratite framing; 87 and 112: pair-cursed body-soul interdependence; 114: female-must-become-male anthropology); or sayings without clear synoptic, Pauline, or Jewish-wisdom corroboration; or short framing/interpretive sayings whose function is internal to the Thomas collection.

- Logion 7 (the lion) — clear Greco-Roman philosophical parallel (Plato Republic IX 588–589 on the soul as multi-headed beast plus lion plus human); no independent Christian or Jewish attestation for any portion.

F. The Gospel of John — Selected Pericopes

The Fourth Gospel, c. 90–110 CE, in its final form. The translation philosophy here treats John as a witness whose narrative bones are reliable but whose theological discourses are the evangelist's voice rather than dominical speech. Included accordingly are: early disciple-calling material, the temple cleansing in its Johannine placement, the Samaritan-woman pericope, the feeding of the five thousand, the healing of the man born blind, the raising of Lazarus, the anointing at Bethany, the footwashing, the Johannine passion narrative in its entirety, the resurrection appearances, and the appendix at the Sea of Tiberias. Excluded as Johannine theological composition rather than Jesus-tradition: the Logos prologue (1:1–18); the Nicodemus discourse content (3:1–21); the bread of life discourse (6:25–71); the discrete egō eimi sayings as theological claims (6:35; 8:12; 10:7, 11; 11:25; 14:6; 15:1); the Good Shepherd discourse (10:1–18); the festival discourses (chs. 7–8, much of 10); the farewell discourses and high-priestly prayer (13:18–17:26).

Johannine conventions: the doublet amēn amēn legō hymin is rendered "Amen, amen, I say to you" (preserving the doublet rather than collapsing to "Truly, truly" or "Verily, verily"); Ioudaioi is rendered "Judeans" rather than "the Jews," following the now-standard scholarly consensus that John's term refers most often to the Judean religious authorities or to inhabitants of Judea, not to the ethno-religious group as a whole; the Johannine historic present is preserved as in the synoptics; sēmeion is rendered "sign," ergon "work," martyria "testimony"; paradidōmi "hand over" as in Mark.

C. Pauline First-Person Appearance Claims (with caveat)

Paul claims to have seen the risen Jesus, and grounds his apostolic authority on this claim. The category-difference between Paul's encounter and the synoptic-gospel appearances is real and worth preserving in any reading: where the synoptic appearances narrate physical-presence encounter (the risen Jesus eats fish, can be touched, is encountered in shared physical space), Paul himself characterizes his encounter as God revealing the Son en emoi — "in me" (Galatians 1:16) — which is the language of internal vision rather than external physical presence. The texts below are included as testimony to a deed of the risen Jesus (appearing) by a source claiming direct witness, while the visionary character of that appearance is named.

Tier 3: Apocryphal Gospels and Fragments — Selected Material

The new criterion opens the door to candidate sources outside the canonical New Testament. Each selection here represents a distinct reliability judgment, made conservatively: material is included where there is genuine scholarly argument for early independent tradition, and excluded where the material is clearly later legend or theological elaboration. Each fragment receives a brief introductory note before its translation describing the source's date, transmission, and the scholarly judgment relevant to its inclusion.

A. Egerton Papyrus 2 — Selected Pericopes

P.Egerton 2 (with the joining fragment P.Köln VI 255) is dated paleographically to the late 2nd century CE; the underlying text is therefore earlier. Bell & Skeat published the editio princeps (Fragments of an Unknown Gospel, 1935); the standard modern edition is in Lührmann, Fragmente apokryph gewordener Evangelien (2000). The fragment preserves four pericopes; three (controversy with religious authorities, healing of a leper, question about tribute) are translated here as candidate testimony with synoptic-style form. The fourth pericope (a fragmentary nature miracle by the Jordan) is excluded as too damaged for reliable translation. The dependence question (early independent tradition vs. 2nd-century composite from the canonical gospels) remains contested; Koester (Ancient Christian Gospels, 1990) and Crossan (Four Other Gospels, 1985) argue for independent early tradition; Wright and Bauckham among others lean toward dependence on the canonical gospels.76

E. The Gospel of Peter — Passion Material Only

The Akhmim fragment (P.Cair. 10759), discovered in 1886/87 in a monk's grave at Akhmim, preserves a substantial portion of the Gospel of Peter narrating the trial, crucifixion, burial, and resurrection. The work is referenced by Eusebius (Hist. eccl. 6.12) as known and used at Rhossus before being suppressed by Bishop Serapion of Antioch (c. 200 CE) on suspicion of docetism. Crossan (The Cross That Spoke, 1988) has argued for an underlying early "Cross Gospel" preserving independent passion tradition; this judgment is contested. Translated here are the elements paralleling synoptic passion narrative (the trial, crucifixion details, burial, and women at the tomb). The talking-cross episode (vv. 35–42), the giant Jesus motif, and the Roman-soldier resurrection-witness narrative are excluded as later legendary embellishment. Translator's Endnotes

The notes below are keyed to the superscript numerals in the body text. They are organized in pericope sequence. Each note documents the lexical range, scholarly literature consulted, grammatical or text-critical issue, or historical-cultural background relevant to the translation choice. These notes are working notes; in many cases the issues raised here are placeholders for fuller treatment in pericope-by-pericope revision.

Notes

  1. euangelion. Mark 1:1. The opening word archē ("beginning") is left literal; whether 1:1 is a title, a thesis-statement, or only the introduction to the John pericope is contested (Marcus, Mark AYB ad loc.; France, Mark NIGTC ad loc.; Collins, Mark Hermeneia ad loc.). Euangelion in pre-Christian Greek (Hellenistic political and imperial usage; Priene calendar inscription, 9 BCE, of Augustus's birthday as euangelion) means "good news/announcement," typically of military victory or imperial event. BDAG s.v. lists "good news" with the imperial-cult background noted. The transliteration with bracketed gloss "[good announcement]" preserves the imperial-resonance the conventional "gospel" has lost; "announcement" is preferred over "news" because in Mark's kairos there is no separation of the message from the formal proclamation. See Hengel, The Four Gospels and the One Gospel (2000), and Stanton, Jesus and Gospel (2004).
  2. Christos and the v.1 text-critical issue. Mark 1:1. Translated "Anointed" per project convention. Christos is a title (translation of Mashiach) and not a proper name in 1:1; the rendering "Christ" obscures this. The phrase "son of God" is omitted in some early witnesses (notably Sinaiticus) and bracketed in NA28 (with brackets indicating the editors' uncertainty); WH includes it. The omission is plausibly accidental (homoioteleuton: χυυυ / χυυυθυυυ), which is the majority text-critical view (Metzger, Textual Commentary*²; Head 1991). Translation follows WH inclusion.
  3. The composite citation. Mark 1:2. Mark attributes to "Isaiah the prophet" what is in fact a composite of Malachi 3:1 and Exodus 23:20 (v.2) followed by Isaiah 40:3 (v.3). This authorial misattribution is preserved in translation per project convention against domesticating it. Some later witnesses (Byzantine textual tradition) "correct" to "in the prophets"; WH and the early uncials read "in Isaiah the prophet." See Marcus, The Way of the Lord (1992), 12–47, on Mark's citation technique.
  4. Isaiah 40:3 / YHWH. Mark 1:3. LXX of Isa 40:3 reads Kyriou, rendering the Tetragrammaton. Per project convention, anarthrous Kyrios in LXX citation is rendered "YHWH" rather than "Lord," with the LXX reading footnoted. The implication that "the way of YHWH" is now to be prepared by John for Jesus is christologically significant and is preserved by this rendering rather than smoothed by the conventional "Lord."
  5. baptisma. Mark 1:4. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[immersion]" on first occurrence per pericope. BDAG s.v. baptisma: "the act of immersion in water in connection with religious purification." The action is total submersion, not aspersion; the cognate verb baptō in classical Greek means "dip, plunge." Conventional "baptism" has become a technical sacramental term in English that obscures the physical action and the connection to Jewish miqveh practice. See Webb, John the Baptizer and Prophet (1991).
  6. metanoia. Mark 1:4. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[turning]." BDAG s.v.: "change of mind/heart." The Hebrew background is šuv ("to turn, return"), which is the LXX standard rendering — šuv in the prophetic literature carries the relational-covenantal sense of returning to YHWH from idolatry. The Latinate "repentance" (from paenitentia, "regret/penance") imports later penitential-system associations the Greek does not have. "Turning" is closer to the underlying Hebraic concept; "change of mind" is too cognitive. See Nave, The Role and Function of Repentance in Luke-Acts (2002).
  7. aphesis. Mark 1:4. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[release]." BDAG s.v. aphesis: "release from captivity, debt, sin, etc." The dominant English "forgiveness" introduces an emotional-relational frame the Greek lacks; aphesis is the LXX rendering of šemittah (Lev 25:10, the Jubilee — "release") and deror (Isa 61:1, "release/liberty"). The legal-debt connotation is primary; the emotional sense is secondary. The cognate verb aphiēmi is rendered "release" throughout. See Hägerland, Jesus and the Forgiveness of Sins (2012), and the foundational Schlatter, Der Glaube im Neuen Testament (1885).
  8. hamartia. Mark 1:4. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[failure]." BDAG s.v.: "failure to meet a standard, sin, transgression." The classical Greek archery image (hamartanō, "to miss the mark") is preserved in "failure" rather than the heavily theological "sin." Louw-Nida 88.289 places it in the domain of "moral and ethical qualities and related behavior." See Lambrecht, "Hamartia in the New Testament" (in his collected essays).
  9. Elijah allusion. Mark 1:6. The description of John's clothing (camel-hair, leather belt) is a deliberate allusion to 2 Kings 1:8 LXX: Elijah is described as anēr dasys with a leather belt around his waist. The eschatological identification of John as Elijah redivivus (cf. Mal 4:5–6/3:23–24 LXX, picked up in Mark 9:11–13) is set in motion at this point. See Öhler, Elia im Neuen Testament (1997).
  10. opisō mou and the strong-one. Mark 1:7. Opisō mou ("after me," "behind me") is also the formula of discipleship (cf. Mark 1:17, opisō mou). The phrase plays double duty: temporally John precedes Jesus, but discipleship-spatially Jesus is Master and John follower. The "stronger-than-me" (ho ischyroteros mou) prepares for 3:27 (the binding of the strong-one). See Marcus, Mark AYB.
  11. pneuma. Mark 1:8. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[breath/spirit]" on first occurrence per pericope. BDAG s.v. pneuma: lists meanings ranging from "wind, breath, spiritual being." The Hebrew ruaḥ and Aramaic ruḥa underlie NT usage; the term oscillates between concrete (wind, breath) and personal (the holy pneuma). English "spirit" has become so disembodied that it loses the concrete physicality the Greek and Hebrew retain. The bracketed gloss preserves the range. See TDNT 6:332–451 (Schweizer).
  12. schizomenous and eis auton. Mark 1:10. The participle schizomenous ("being-torn-apart") is a violent image (cf. Mark 15:38, the temple curtain "torn"); the heavens are split, not gently opened (Matthew and Luke domesticate to aneōchthēsan, "were opened"). The preposition eis with the dove is "into" rather than the conventional "upon" — Mark's Greek says the pneuma descends into Jesus, not just upon him; this is theologically important for any "adoptionist" reading of the verse. See Marcus, Mark AYB; Bird, Jesus the Eternal Son (2017), for an alternative reading.
  13. Wilderness, beasts, and Satan. Mark 1:13. The Markan testing pericope is far more compressed than the Q version (cf. Lk 4:1–13 / Mt 4:1–11). The presence of the thēria ("wild-beasts") is unique to Mark; possible echoes include Adam in Eden (Gen 2:19–20) and Isaiah 11:6–9 (eschatological peace among beasts). See Bauckham, "Jesus and the Wild Animals (Mark 1:13)" (1994).
  14. paradothēnai. Mark 1:14. Paradidōmi — "hand over, deliver up" — is a Markan keyword; it appears here of John's arrest, and recurs of Judas's betrayal of Jesus (3:19, 14:10) and of Jesus's being handed over by God (9:31, 10:33). The passive without explicit agent ("was handed-over") may be a divine passive. See Popkes, Christus Traditus (1967).
  15. basileia. Mark 1:15. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[reign]" per project decision. The conventional "kingdom" implies a territorial-political entity; basileia is primarily an abstract noun denoting the act of reigning, kingship, rule — though it can also mean the realm. BDAG s.v. basileia lists three senses: (1) kingship, royal power, royal rule; (2) territory ruled by a king; (3) the realm of the heavenly. The dynamic sense is primary in Jesus's usage. See Beasley-Murray, Jesus and the Kingdom of God (1986); Caragounis, "Kingdom of God / Kingdom of Heaven" in DJG² (2013).
  16. pistis. Mark 1:15. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[trust]" per project decision. BDAG s.v. pistis: "faith, trust, faithfulness." The English "faith" has become a religious-special term; pistis in Greek is ordinary — what one has in a friend, a doctor, a contract. The Septuagintal background of pistis renders Hebrew 'emunah (faithfulness, steadfastness) and 'aman (to trust, believe). "Trust" preserves both directionality (trust in) and quality (trustworthy). On the contested pistis Christou construction in Paul, see preface and commentary on the pre-Pauline Romans/Galatians passages where it occurs. See Hays, The Faith of Jesus Christ² (2002), and on the wider lexical question, Morgan, Roman Faith and Christian Faith (2015).
  17. Fishers of human-beings. Mark 1:17. Halieis anthrōpōn; literally "fishers of human-beings." The phrase is jarring; the OT background (Jer 16:16, Ezek 29:4–5, Amos 4:2) is largely negative — fishing for humans is judgment imagery. Whether Mark has reframed it as positive call-to-mission, or whether the dark resonance survives, is debated. See Wuellner, The Meaning of "Fishers of Men" (1967).
  18. Ti hēmin kai soi. Mark 1:24. Hebraic idiom (mah-li valakh, "what to me and to you?") = "what have we to do with each other?" / "leave me alone." LXX precedent at Judg 11:12, 2 Sam 16:10, 1 Kings 17:18, 2 Kings 3:13. BDF §127. Translated literally per project convention to preserve the Semitism.
  19. christon einai variant. Mark 1:34. Some witnesses add Christon einai ("[that he is] the Anointed") at the end of v.34. WH brackets; NA28 omits. Translation follows WH brackets, marked as bracketed.
  20. splanchnistheis / embrimēsamenos. Mark 1:41. Significant text-critical crux: WH and the majority of witnesses read splanchnistheis ("being-moved-in-the-bowels"); D and a small group of witnesses read orgistheis ("being-angered"). The "harder reading" canon argues for orgistheis (why would a scribe make Jesus angry?), and several recent commentators (e.g., Ehrman, Misquoting Jesus and The Orthodox Corruption of Scripture) prefer it; others (e.g., Metzger, defenders of WH) preserve splanchnistheis. Translation here follows WH. Bowel-imagery is the Hebraic seat of compassion (cf. raḥamim, "compassion," from reḥem, "womb"); the literal "moved in the bowels" preserves the visceral concreteness. The growling at v.43 (embrimēsamenos) preserves the abrupt, jarring Markan tone regardless of the v.41 reading.
  21. pistis at 2:5. Note that the pistis attributed is autōn ("their" — the four bearers), not the paralytic's. The faith effecting healing here is not the patient's. See France, Mark NIGTC ad loc.
  22. kardia. Mark 2:6, 8. Translated "heart" per project convention. The Hebraic lev/lebab is the seat of will, intellect, and decision — not primarily of emotion as in modern English. Where the heart "reasons" (as here), the Hebraic background is operative. The bracketed gloss "[hearts]" is suppressed after first occurrence per convention. See TDNT 3:605–614 (Behm).
  23. Son of the human-being. Mark 2:10. First Markan occurrence of huios tou anthrōpou. Translated "the son of the human-being" per project convention against the conventional "Son of Man." The phrase translates Aramaic bar-enasha, an idiom meaning either "human being" generically, "I/this person" idiomatically, or — in the Danielic apocalyptic stream (Dan 7:13) — a specific eschatological figure. Mark uses the phrase in three contexts: (1) Jesus's earthly authority (here, 2:28), (2) suffering predictions (8:31, 9:31, 10:33), (3) future glorious coming (8:38, 13:26, 14:62). The contested question is whether Jesus uses it idiomatically (Vermes, Jesus the Jew [1973]) or titularly (Hurtado, Lord Jesus Christ [2003]); most likely both are operative. Daniel 7:13 connection: the figure who comes "with the clouds of heaven" to receive everlasting dominion.
  24. hamartōloi. Mark 2:15. Translated "failed-ones" per the hamartia convention. The category in Second Temple Judaism is more sociological than moral: "sinners" are those who do not maintain the purity codes of the Pharisees, including most ordinary working people, tax-collectors, prostitutes, etc. See Sanders, Jesus and Judaism (1985), 174–211; Dunn, Jesus Remembered (2003), 526–531.
  25. dikaios. Mark 2:17. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[just-ones]" on first occurrence. The dik- word group (BDAG s.v. dikaios, dikaiosynē, dikaioō) carries the dual sense of "just" (legal/social) and "righteous" (religious/ethical) — English bifurcates what Greek and Hebrew (tsadiq) keep unified. "Just-ones" preserves the legal/social register; "righteous" sounds piously religious in modern English. See Ziesler, The Meaning of Righteousness in Paul (1972); Westerholm, Justification Reconsidered (2013); and Wolter, Paulus (2011) for the contested Pauline rendering.
  26. Abiathar / Ahimelech. Mark 2:26. Mark says David ate the bread of presence "in the days of Abiathar the chief-priest." 1 Samuel 21:1–6, the source story, has Ahimelech, not Abiathar (Abiathar is Ahimelech's son, who escapes Saul's slaughter at 22:20 and serves David later). This is an authorial slip preserved per project convention. Matthew (12:3–4) and Luke (6:3–4) drop the name entirely. See Marcus, Mark AYB, ad loc.
  27. psychē. Mark 3:4. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[life/self]" on first occurrence per pericope. BDAG s.v. psychē: "life, soul, self." The dichotomy of "soul vs body" in modern English is heavily Platonic; psychē in the NT and LXX (rendering Hebrew nephesh) is closer to "life-force," "self," "person." It can mean physical life (as here, "to save psychē or to kill"); the inner self; or simply the person. See Bauckham, "Life, Death, and the Afterlife in Second Temple Judaism," in Eschatology of the New Testament; TDNT 9:608–660 (Schweizer et al.).
  28. apostolos. Mark 3:14, 6:30. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[sent-ones]" on first occurrence. Apostolos (cognate of apostellō, "to send") in classical and Hellenistic Greek means "envoy, messenger, delegated representative." The Hebraic šaliaḥ (the legal-emissary) underlies the NT specialized usage. "Apostle" has become so technical-ecclesiastical that the meaning of mission/delegation is obscured. See Agnew, "The Origin of the NT Apostle-Concept" (1986); Hahn, Mission in the New Testament (1965).
  29. exestē and par' autou. Mark 3:21. Hoi par' autou ("those-from-him") is ambiguous: "his family/relatives" (most likely) or "his disciples." Exestē (perfect of existēmi) literally "to stand outside oneself," idiomatically "to be out of one's mind, beside oneself." The rendering "beside-himself" preserves the Greek idiom. See Marcus, Mark AYB, ad loc., for the patristic and modern discussion.
  30. The strong-one bound. Mark 3:27. Echoes Isaiah 49:24–25; cf. the wider Second Temple tradition of binding evil powers (1 Enoch 10:4 binding of Asael; Test. Levi 18; Q 11:21–22 / Mt 12:29 / Lk 11:21–22 parallel). See Twelftree, Jesus the Exorcist (1993).
  31. aiōn / aiōnios. Mark 3:29. Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[of-the-age]" per project decision. BDAG s.v. aiōn: "very long time, eternity, age." The Greek term is fundamentally temporal (an age, an epoch); the leap to "everlasting/eternal" in Latinate translation tradition imports a metaphysical-spatial sense the Greek does not require. The Hebraic 'olam underlies LXX aiōn and similarly carries the sense of "horizon/age" rather than abstract eternity. The "hamartia aiōnios" is most likely "a failure-act characterizing this age," not an abstractly "eternal sin." See Ramelli & Konstan, Terms for Eternity (2007); on the eschatological "two-aiōn" framework, see Wright, The New Testament and the People of God (1992).
  32. mystērion. Mark 4:11. Mystērion (LXX of Daniel translates Aramaic raz; cf. Qumran raz nihyeh, "the mystery to-be") is "a hidden purpose disclosed by revelation," not "mystery" in the modern sense of the unknown. See TDNT 4:802–828 (Bornkamm); Brown, The Semitic Background of the Term "Mystery" in the NT (1968).
  33. Isaiah 6 citation. Mark 4:12. Mark's citation of Isa 6:9–10 in v.12 follows the Targum rather than the LXX or the MT — specifically the mēpote clause, "lest they should turn and it be released to them," matches Targum Isa 6:10's "and it be forgiven them." This is the Markan text's most distinctive feature here. See Marcus, The Way of the Lord, 95–110.
  34. pistis in healing formula. Mark 5:34. The formula hē pistis sou sesōken se ("your pistis has saved you") recurs at 10:52 (Bartimaeus). The verb sōzō in healing contexts means "heal/rescue" (BDAG s.v. sōzō sense 2); "save" is overloaded religiously. Some translations choose "made you well." Translation here keeps "saved" because the same verb later carries the eschatological-theological sense, and the ambiguity is part of the text. See Wells, The Greek Language of Healing from Homer to New Testament Times (1998).
  35. Talitha koum. Mark 5:41. Aramaic preserved in transliteration with the Greek translation following — Mark's standard practice when preserving Aramaic of Jesus (cf. Ephphatha 7:34, Abba 14:36, Eloi eloi lama sabachthani 15:34). The presence of these Aramaic phrases is an important historical-tradition marker; see Casey, Aramaic Sources of Mark's Gospel (1998); Meier, A Marginal Jew, vol. 1, chs. 7–8.
  36. Egō eimi. Mark 6:50. Literally "I am" (or "It is I"). The phrase is also the LXX rendering of the divine self-revelation (Exod 3:14, Isa 43:10, etc.). Whether Mark intends the theophanic resonance here is debated; the Johannine egō eimi sayings make the resonance unambiguous. Translation preserves the Greek in transliteration to leave the question open. See Williams, I Am He: The Interpretation of 'Anî Hû' in Jewish and Early Christian Literature (2000).
  37. hypokritēs. Mark 7:6. Per project decision, rendered "play-actor" (with the transliteration preserved on first occurrence per pericope) rather than the modern English "hypocrite." BDAG s.v.: "actor, pretender." The classical Greek meaning is theatrical: an actor performing a role; the modern English "hypocrite" has narrowed to mean someone who professes virtues they do not have. The theatrical metaphor preserves Mark's edge: the Pharisees are performing piety, not living it. See TDNT 8:559–571 (Wilckens).
  38. Kyrie as ordinary address. Mark 7:28. The Syrophoenician woman addresses Jesus kyrie, ordinary polite address ("sir"). Per project convention this is rendered contextually — "kyrie — sir" — rather than the conventional "Lord," because she is a Greek-speaking gentile who has no necessary christological commitment. See preface on the Kyrios convention.
  39. kosmos. Mark 8:36. Kosmos in Greek originally means "order, arrangement"; secondarily "the world, the inhabited universe." In NT usage (especially Johannine and Pauline), it can carry the negative sense of "the present age in opposition to God." Here the sense is "the world" as the whole of created reality one might gain. See TDNT 3:868–898 (Sasse).
  40. doxa. Mark 8:38, 10:37, 13:26. Doxa renders Hebrew kavod in LXX — "weight, glory, manifest presence." The classical Greek doxa meant "opinion, reputation, what is thought," and the cognate dokeō preserves this sense. The LXX captures kavod with doxa, expanding the semantic range; the NT inherits the LXX usage. See TDNT 2:232–255 (Kittel/von Rad).
  41. Gehenna. Mark 9:43–48. The Hebrew Ge-Hinnom (Valley of Hinnom) — south of Jerusalem, site of child-sacrifice in monarchic times (2 Kgs 23:10, Jer 7:31, 19:5–6) — became in late Second Temple imagery the place of eschatological judgment by fire. Translation preserves the Aramaic-Hebraic Gehenna against the conventional "hell," which has accrued unrelated medieval connotations. The citation in v.48 is from Isa 66:24. See Bailey, "Gehenna: The Topography of Hell" (1986).
  42. One sarx. Mark 10:8. Citation of Gen 2:24 LXX. Sarx is "flesh" (BDAG s.v. sense 1); the broader Pauline sense of "fallen humanity" is later. Here it is the basic anthropological sense.
  43. zōē aiōnios. Mark 10:17, 30. Literally "life of-the-age." Per the aiōn convention, this is left as "life of-the-age" rather than "eternal life." The phrase translates rabbinic ḥayye 'olam ha-ba — "life of the age-to-come" — and refers in its earliest stratum to participation in the eschatological resurrection-age, not abstract immortality. See Wright, The Resurrection of the Son of God (2003).
  44. Lytron. Mark 10:45. Lytron (BDAG s.v.) means "ransom, price of release"; the cognate verb lytroō means "to ransom, redeem." LXX renders various Hebrew terms (ge'ullah, kofer, padah) with this word-group. The Markan v.45 is widely held to be one of the most contested verses in Markan studies — whether it is authentic Jesus tradition (Jeremias, New Testament Theology, 292–294, defends authenticity) or a later soteriological development (Hooker, The Gospel According to St Mark, 248–250, is more cautious). See also Page, "The Authenticity of the Ransom Logion" (1980).
  45. Den of bandits. Mark 11:17. Composite citation: Isa 56:7 (the inclusion of gentiles in the Temple) + Jer 7:11 (the prophetic indictment of the Temple as a "den of bandits"). The word lēstēs (cf. Mark 14:48) is "bandit/insurgent," with the political-revolutionary connotations Josephus uses for the Sicarii. See Marcus, The Way of the Lord, 111–129; Bauckham, "Jesus's Demonstration in the Temple" (1988).
  46. The son does not know. Mark 13:32. A theologically delicate verse: the Markan Jesus explicitly does not know "the day or the hour"; ignorance is attributed to the huios. Some witnesses (and Matthew 24:36 in part of the manuscript tradition) omit the phrase "nor the son" — almost certainly a theologically motivated correction. WH retains; translation follows. See Kelhoffer, "The Reception of Mark 13:32" (2010).
  47. sōma. Mark 14:22, in eucharistic formula. Sōma — "body" — is distinct from sarx (flesh). The bracketed gloss is suppressed after first occurrence per convention. In the eucharistic context, the Aramaic underlying may be guphi or bisrî; if bisrî, this would be sarx not sōma, but the Markan Greek tradition has sōma. See Jeremias, The Eucharistic Words of Jesus (³1966).
  48. diathēkē. Mark 14:24. Diathēkē in classical Greek primarily means "last will/testament"; in the LXX it consistently renders Hebrew berit ("covenant"). The NT uses the LXX-flavored sense of "covenant," but the testamentary connotations are exploited in Hebrews. Here Mark is in the LXX-Hebraic register: "blood of the covenant," echoing Exod 24:8. See Kutsch, Verheissung und Gesetz (1973); Hahn, Old Testament in the New: An Introduction (2002).
  49. Abba. Mark 14:36. The Aramaic preserved per project convention with the Greek translation following ("the Father"). Abba is the determinate Aramaic form ("the Father"); whether it carries any special intimacy ("Daddy"), as Jeremias once argued, has been substantially qualified by later scholarship. It is not childish address; it is the determinate form, which is also the form of polite/respectful address. See Barr, "Abbā Isn't Daddy" (1988); D'Angelo, "Abba and 'Father'" (1992); Hurtado, How on Earth Did Jesus Become a God? (2005), 99–110.
  50. sarx. Mark 14:38. Sarx — "flesh" — here in the contrast with pneuma approaches the later Pauline sense of frail/fallen humanity, but is not yet identical to it. The bracketed gloss "[flesh]" is presented on first occurrence per pericope. See TDNT 7:98–151 (Schweizer); Dunn, The Theology of Paul the Apostle (1998), 62–73.
  51. The Eloi cry. Mark 15:34. Citation of Psalm 22:1 (LXX 21:2). Mark transliterates an Aramaic / mixed-Aramaic-Hebrew form (Elōi is Aramaic; lama is Hebrew/Aramaic; sabachthani is Aramaic). Matthew "corrects" toward Hebrew (Ēli). The bystanders' confusion of Elōi with "Elijah" (v.35) makes more phonetic sense with the Aramaic. See Casey, Aramaic Sources of Mark's Gospel, 83–93.
  52. The temple curtain. Mark 15:38. The participle eschisthē ("was torn") echoes 1:10 (schizomenous — the heavens being torn at the immersion). The inclusio is structurally important and is usually missed by translation that smooths the verb. Whether the curtain in question is the inner parōkhet (separating Holy of Holies from Holy Place) or the outer masakh is contested. See Gurtner, The Torn Veil (2007).
  53. The ending at 16:8. Mark 16:8. The original gospel ends here, with the women "saying nothing to no one, for they were afraid" (ephobounto gar). The longer ending (16:9–20) and the shorter ending are both 2nd-century additions, not present in Sinaiticus, Vaticanus, the Old Latin tradition, or the early Syriac. WH brackets; NA28 brackets. The grammatical ephobounto gar — ending a sentence with gar — is unusual but not impossible in Greek. Per project convention, the longer ending is excluded. See Croy, The Mutilation of Mark's Gospel (2003); Kelhoffer, Miracle and Mission (2000).
  54. The Slanderer. Q 2 (Lk 4:1–13 par.). Diabolos — literally "slanderer" or "one who throws across" — is the LXX rendering of Hebrew śaṭān ("accuser"). Per the project's preference for unmasking the underlying lexical sense, "Slanderer" is used rather than the conventional "Devil," which has accreted unrelated medieval imagery. Satanas (transliteration of Aramaic saṭana) is preserved as "Satan" per convention.
  55. dikaiosynē. Q 3 (Mt 5:6, 5:10). Transliterated with bracketed gloss "[justice]" on first occurrence per pericope. Per the dik- family convention. The conventional "righteousness" obscures the social-legal force; in Matthew especially, the term carries both the senses of personal piety and social justice. See Przybylski, Righteousness in Matthew and His World of Thought (1980).
  56. agapaō. Q 4 (Lk 6:27). Per the project's preference for not collapsing the love-vocabulary, agapaō / agapē is rendered "love" with awareness that philia, erōs, and storgē would require different treatment in passages where they appear together or in contrast. The over-theologized "agapē = self-giving love" is a Nygren-era simplification (Nygren, Agape and Eros [1932]); the classical-Hellenistic semantic field is more diffuse. See Joly, Le vocabulaire chrétien de l'amour (1968); Spicq, Agapè dans le Nouveau Testament (1958–59).
  57. euangelizō. Q 9 (Lk 7:22). Cognate of euangelion; rendered "to good-news" (passive: "are good-news-ed") to preserve the linkage. See note 1.
  58. The evil-one in the LP. Q 15 / Mt 6:13. Apo tou ponērou — "from the evil[-one]" — is grammatically ambiguous: the genitive can be neuter ("evil") or masculine personal ("the evil-one," i.e., the Diabolos). NT usage favors the personal reading (cf. Mt 13:19, 38; 1 John 2:13–14). Translation here follows the personal reading. See Brown, "The Pater Noster as an Eschatological Prayer" (1961); Carmignac, Recherches sur le Notre Père (1969).
  59. mamōna. Q 34 (Lk 16:13 / Mt 6:24). Aramaic mamōn preserved in transliteration. Mamōna in Aramaic is "wealth, property, possessions"; not a personified deity, but here functioning by metonymy as a competitor for service. See Hauck, TDNT 4:388–390.
  60. Luke 23:34a — Father, release them. The first word from the cross in Luke ("Father, release to them; for they do not know what they do") is textually disputed. WH includes; NA28 brackets it as questionable; some early witnesses (P75, B, D, W) omit. The omission is probably theologically motivated (anti-Jewish polemic), though Ehrman, The Orthodox Corruption of Scripture (1993), argues for the textual reading either way. Translation preserves the verse with brackets per project convention for marking textual uncertainty. See Metzger, Textual Commentary²; Ehrman, "The Text of the Gospels at the End of the Second Century" in Studies in the Textual Criticism of the New Testament*.
  61. Coptic Thomas — translation conventions. The Coptic of Nag Hammadi II,2 is a translation from Greek (we have fragmentary Greek originals at P.Oxy 1, 654, 655). Specific Coptic features preserved in this translation: (1) nomina sacra (e.g., ⲓ︤ⲥ︥ for Iēsous) are rendered as the full name; (2) the standard introduction peje IS ("Jesus said") is preserved; (3) the Coptic tmntero (kingdom) is rendered "basileia" — though strictly speaking this is a back-translation, since the Coptic translator's choice reflects the underlying Greek basileia; (4) bracketed lacunae and angle-bracket editorial supplements are preserved where they affect translation. The text-base is Layton 1989 (Nag Hammadi Codex II,2–7, Brill). See DeConick, The Original Gospel of Thomas in Translation (2006); Plisch, The Gospel of Thomas: Original Text with Commentary (2008); Gathercole, The Gospel of Thomas: Introduction and Commentary (2014).
  62. Messias / Christos. John 1:41. The Johannine narrator's translation aside (Hebrew/Aramaic Messias → Greek Christos) is preserved literally: "Messiah — which is, being-translated, Anointed." This makes visible what conventional translation flattens — that Christos is an anointing-translation of Mashiach, not an independent name. The same translation aside recurs at John 4:25. See Hurtado, Lord Jesus Christ (2003), 98–101; on John's use of these translation asides as a feature of his narration to a Greek-speaking but Semitism-aware audience, see Bauckham, The Testimony of the Beloved Disciple (2007).
  63. Ioudaioi. John 2:13 ff. (recurring throughout John). Translated "Judeans" rather than the conventional "the Jews" per the now-standard scholarly reframing of John's distinctive usage. The post-Holocaust history of John's Ioudaioi receiving a flattening anti-Semitic reading has motivated a reconsideration of the Johannine usage in its 1st-century setting. The most common Johannine sense is the Judean religious authorities (especially Temple-aligned), or "Judeans" in geographic-sectarian distinction from Galileans and Samaritans, not the ethno-religious group as a whole. The translation "Judeans" preserves this geographic-political-sectarian range. See Lowe, "Who Were the Ioudaioi?" (1976); Reinhartz, Befriending the Beloved Disciple (2001); Esler, "Ioudaioi" in Conflict and Identity in Romans (2003); cf. the contrary view in Frey & Schnelle (eds.), Kontexte des Johannesevangeliums (2004).
  64. Egō eimi in John 4:26. Although the project translates the John material conservatively and excludes the discrete egō eimi discourses (6:35; 8:12; etc.), the egō eimi phrase recurs in narrative material that is included. Here at 4:26 the formula is in the response to the Samaritan woman; the rendering "I am — egō eimi — the speaking to you" preserves the Greek without forcing the theophanic reading. Whether the Johannine narrative egō eimi (here, and at 18:5–8) carries the LXX divine-self-revelation echo (cf. Exod 3:14, Isa 43:10) is contested but live. See Williams, I Am He (2000); Ball, 'I Am' in John's Gospel (1996).
  65. Siloam pun. John 9:7. The narrator's translation aside ("Siloam — which is being-translated, Sent") is theologically loaded: Jesus is the Sent-one in Johannine theology (cf. 5:36, 6:29, etc.), and the man washes in the pool of the Sent-one, who is also the one who sent him there. The aside is preserved as in the Greek; the wordplay on Šiloah (Hebrew "sent") and apostellō / pempō is a Johannine feature. See Brown, John AYB i, 380; Moloney, The Gospel of John (1998), 295–296.
  66. Annas and Caiaphas. John 18:13–14. The note that Annas was Caiaphas's father-in-law is unique to John in the canonical gospels and constitutes precisely the kind of incidental historical detail that supports the canon's judgment that John is reliable for passion-narrative material. Annas (Ananos b. Sethi) was high priest 6–15 CE, deposed by Valerius Gratus; Caiaphas (Yosef b. Caiapha) was his son-in-law, high priest 18–36 CE (so Josephus, Ant. 18.2.1, 18.4.3). Annas continued to wield enormous influence even after his deposition; five of his sons and his son-in-law all served as high priest in succession. See Bond, Caiaphas: Friend of Rome and Judge of Jesus? (2004); Bauckham, Jesus and the Eyewitnesses (²2017), 393–400.
  67. The trilingual titulus. John 19:20. The titulus inscribed in three languages (Hebrew, Latin, Greek) is unique to John. The order of languages varies in textual witnesses; WH reads Hebrew–Latin–Greek. Latin is the imperial-administrative language; Greek is the lingua franca of the eastern Roman world; Hebrew (or Aramaic — hebraisti in John typically means Aramaic) is the local Judean language. The historicity of trilingual public inscriptions in Roman-period Jerusalem is supported by epigraphic evidence (cf. the Temple-warning inscriptions in Greek and Latin, the Theodotus inscription). See Brown, Death of the Messiah (1994), 962–968.
  68. Thomas's confession. John 20:28. Ho kyrios mou kai ho theos mou — "my Kyrios and my God." The most explicit identification of Jesus as theos in the synoptic-narrative tradition (excluding the Logos prologue, which falls outside this canon's scope). Whether this is direct theological confession addressed to Jesus, or an exclamation of astonishment with Jesus and God as separate addressees, is contested but the grammatical default is direct address. The doublet kyrios + theos echoes LXX usage (rendering YHWH + Elohim). See Harris, Jesus as God (1992), 105–129; Bauckham, Jesus and the God of Israel (2008), 248–250.
  69. Agapaō / phileō in John 21:15–17. The threefold restoration of Peter alternates the verbs agapaō (vv. 15, 16) and phileō (vv. 15, 16, 17), with Jesus shifting to phileō at v. 17. Whether this is a meaningful semantic distinction (so the older patristic reading: agapē as divine self-giving, philia as human friendship) or stylistic variation (so most recent commentators: Brown, Carson, Köstenberger) is contested. The translation preserves the Greek both verbs to leave the question open; per project convention against collapsing the love-vocabulary, the variation is preserved. See Spicq, Agapè dans le Nouveau Testament (1958–59); against the strong distinction, Carson, The Gospel According to John (1991), 676–677.
  70. Paradidōmi in 1 Corinthians 11:23. The double use of paradidōmi in 1 Cor 11:23 — Paul "received" (parelabon) and "handed-over" (paredōka) the tradition; Jesus was being "handed-over" (paredideto) — preserves the Markan keyword. Whether paredideto refers to Judas's betrayal (as in the synoptic passion tradition) or to God's handing-over of the Son (as in the Pauline soteriological usage; cf. Rom 8:32) is debated; the passive without explicit agent allows both. See Popkes, Christus Traditus (1967); Wolter, Paulus (2011), 215–220.
  71. Ektrōma. 1 Corinthians 15:8. Ektrōma (BDAG s.v.) literally means "miscarriage, abortion, untimely birth." Paul applies it to himself. Whether the term is self-deprecation (the "least" born among the apostles, the disgraceful one), or a conventional polemical insult that Paul has reclaimed (Schwartz argued opponents had used it of him), or a temporal marker (he was "born out of due time" — born after the appointed appearance period had ended) is contested. Translation here as "the untimely-born" preserves the temporal sense without committing on the polemical question. See Munck, "Paulus tamquam abortivus" (1959); Nickelsburg, "An ektrōma, though appointed from the womb" (1986).
  72. En emoi. Galatians 1:16. The most theologically consequential prepositional phrase in Pauline studies. Apokalypsai ton huion autou en emoi — "to reveal his son in me." The dative emoi with en admits at least three readings: (1) locative-internal: "in me" = within my consciousness/heart, the language of internal vision and revelation; (2) instrumental: "through me" = "in [the case of] me," Paul as instrument of revelation to others; (3) dative of advantage: "to me," with en as relatively colorless. The locative-internal reading is supported by Paul's contrast with sarx kai haimati in v. 16b ("flesh and blood" = external/physical witness) and by the parallel in v. 12 (di' apokalypseōs — "through revelation"). This reading produces the visionary-encounter caveat that the Jesus Tradition canon foregrounds: Paul's "appearance" is structurally distinct in kind from the synoptic appearance narratives where the risen Jesus is encountered in shared physical space. See Segal, Paul the Convert (1990); Kim, The Origin of Paul's Gospel (²1984); Hurtado, Lord Jesus Christ (2003), 99–101 and 175–177.
  73. The agraphon. Acts 20:35. Makarion estin mallon didonai ē lambanein — "blessed it is rather to give than to receive." The most famous of the dominical agrapha (sayings of Jesus not preserved in the four canonical gospels). Possible parallels: the proverb is paralleled in classical sources (Aristotle, Thucydides, Plutarch all preserve similar maxims), so it may be a Hellenistic proverb that Jesus quoted or used; alternatively, it may be a genuine logion of Jesus shaped by Hellenistic proverbial form. The agraphon is preserved by Luke (writing c. 80–85 CE) as remembered by Paul, who would have learned it from Jerusalem tradition. See Jeremias, Unknown Sayings of Jesus (²1964), 76–82; Hofius, "Isolated Sayings of Jesus" in Schneemelcher, New Testament Apocrypha (1991), 1:88–91.
  74. The gathered ekklēsia. Didache 9:4. The image of grain scattered on the mountains and gathered into one loaf is liturgical-eschatological: the dispersed ekklēsia gathered into the basileia in a single eucharistic body. The image plays on the bread itself as a unifying physical fact. The earliest form of the ekklēsia concept in the Didache is local-assembly (ekklēsia = "gathered assembly," from kaleō "to call" and ek "out"); not yet "Church" in the institutional sense. See Niederwimmer, The Didache (Hermeneia, 1998), 144–146; Milavec, The Didache: Faith, Hope, and Life of the Earliest Christian Communities (2003).
  75. Maran atha. Didache 10:6. Aramaic acclamation: māranā' tā ("our Lord, come!" — imperative) or māran 'ătā ("our Lord has come" — perfect). Cf. 1 Cor 16:22, where Paul preserves the same Aramaic (without translation), confirming early liturgical use. Translation preserves the Aramaic in transliteration per project convention. See Fitzmyer, "New Testament Kyrios and Maranatha" (1971); Hurtado, Lord Jesus Christ, 110–112.
  76. Egerton Papyrus 2 — dependence question. The fragment dates paleographically to c. 200 CE, providing a terminus ante quem. The text it transmits is therefore earlier (mid-2nd century at the latest), but how much earlier is contested. Koester, Ancient Christian Gospels (1990), 205–216, argues that Egerton preserves an early stratum of Jesus tradition independent of the canonical gospels, partly on the basis that the controversy about authority and the leper-healing pericope show synoptic-style rather than synoptic-derivative form. Crossan, Four Other Gospels (1985), supports independence. Wright, The Resurrection of the Son of God (2003), 593–594, treats Egerton as 2nd-century composite. Foster, "The Gospel of Egerton 2" (in Foster ed., The Non-Canonical Gospels, 2008), surveys the debate. The leper pericope's distinctive line — the leper says he became a leper from journeying-with and eating-with lepers in an inn — is unparalleled in synoptic tradition and is hard to derive from canonical sources, suggesting at least some independent material.
  77. P.Oxy 840 — Temple purity controversy. A small parchment leaf with one extended pericope. Discovered by Grenfell and Hunt in 1905, published 1908. The pericope reads as authentic synoptic-style controversy material: a Pharisee chief priest named Levi challenges Jesus and his disciples for entering the Temple's purity-zone without immersion; Jesus's response (about the inner uncleanness vs. outer washing, with the harsh comparison to harlots) parallels Mark 7:1–23 in spirit but is not derivable from it textually. Some scholars (Schwemer, Kruger) defend authenticity of an early Jesus tradition; others (Bovon, Schröter) treat it as 2nd-century composition. The Temple ritual-bath vocabulary (loutron, "pool of David," ascending and descending staircases) reflects detailed knowledge of Second Temple Jewish purity practice — either authentic memory or 2nd-century composition by someone with that knowledge. See Kruger, The Gospel of the Savior (2005); Bovon, "Fragment Oxyrhynchus 840" (2000).
  78. Gospel of Peter — passion fragment. The Akhmim manuscript (P.Cair. 10759) is an 8th-century copy of an earlier text. The Gospel of Peter as Eusebius knew it (referenced in Hist. eccl. 6.12) was used at Rhossus until Bishop Serapion of Antioch (c. 200 CE) suppressed it as docetic. The work is therefore 2nd-century. Crossan, The Cross That Spoke (1988), argues that the Gospel of Peter contains an underlying earlier "Cross Gospel" preserving independent passion tradition; this judgment is contested by Brown, Death of the Messiah (1994), 1317–1349, and Foster, The Gospel of Peter (2010), who treat the work as a 2nd-century composite drawing on canonical passion narratives with embellishment. The talking-cross episode (vv. 35–42, where the cross emerging from the tomb answers from heaven, "Yes") and the giant-Jesus motif (where the heads of the resurrection witnesses reach to heaven and Jesus's head exceeds them) are excluded as later legendary elements not consistent with the synoptic-style passion material.
  79. Origen's agraphon from the Gospel of the Hebrews. Ho thaumasas basileusei kai ho basileusas anapauēsetai — "He that wonders shall reign, and he that has-reigned shall rest." Cited by Origen, Comm. Matt. 15.14 (commentary on Mt 19:16–19). The same saying appears in Greek P.Oxy 654 (= Thomas 2 in the Greek tradition) in the form: "Let him who seeks not cease seeking until he finds; and finding he shall be troubled, and being-troubled he shall marvel, and he shall reign over the all." The Coptic Thomas 2 has only the first three clauses (seek-find-be-troubled), suggesting that the version Origen knew preserves an additional kingdom-and-rest clause that has dropped out of the Coptic. The independent attestation across Origen's GHeb citation and the Greek P.Oxy 654 supports the saying's early circulation. See Klijn, Jewish-Christian Gospel Tradition (1992), 47–55.
  80. Resurrection appearance to James (Jerome, De Viris Illustribus 2). Jerome's Latin paraphrase of (he claims) the Aramaic-or-Hebrew Gospel of the Hebrews is the only narrative we have of the appearance to James, the brother of Jesus, that is referenced in 1 Corinthians 15:7. The narrative has James swearing not to eat bread until the resurrection, then receiving Jesus's appearance and bread broken in his hand. Whether Jerome is faithfully transmitting the GHeb or expanding from limited source material is debated; he reports having translated the GHeb himself (claim of De Vir. Ill. 3) but his other references to the gospel are not always self-consistent. The pre-Gospel-of-Hebrews tradition behind the appearance to James is plausibly very early (cf. the early date of the 1 Cor 15 formula and the prominence of James in the Jerusalem church per Acts and Galatians). See Klijn, Jewish-Christian Gospel Tradition, 91–94; Bauckham, Jude and the Relatives of Jesus (1990), 79–110.
  81. The Ebionite baptism account (Epiphanius, Panarion 30.13.7–8). Epiphanius preserves what he claims is the Ebionite gospel's account of Jesus's baptism. The narrative is theologically distinctive: a pluriform heavenly voice that includes the Western variant egō sēmeron gegennēka se — "I today have-begotten you" (cf. Codex Bezae at Luke 3:22, citing Ps 2:7) — and a great light that shines around the place at the moment of the pneuma's descent. The light-at-the-baptism motif is paralleled in the Diatessaron tradition and in Justin Martyr (Dial. 88), suggesting it is an early element of baptism tradition that the canonical evangelists did not retain. The Ebionite version is doctrinally tendentious (the group's adoptionist Christology is at stake), but the baptism material may preserve independent tradition shaped to that theology. See Klijn, Jewish-Christian Gospel Tradition, 65–71; Verheyden, "The Ebionite Gospel" (in Apokryph gewordene Schriften, 2018).
  82. Logion 2 (Seeking and finding). The saying is multiply-attested in three independent transmission streams: the Coptic of Thomas (NHC II,2), the Greek of P.Oxy 654 (which preserves a slightly fuller form including the line "and having reigned, he shall rest"), and Origen's citation of the Gospel of the Hebrews (Commentary on Matthew 15.14). The four-step progression — seeking → finding → being-troubled → marveling → reigning (with P.Oxy 654 adding the fifth step, resting) — is structurally a wisdom-saying form found in Jewish wisdom literature (cf. Wis 6:12–14; Sirach 6:18–37). The independent multiple attestation across language streams and literary genres is exceptionally strong evidence for early traceable tradition. See Patterson, The Gospel of Thomas and Christian Origins (2013), 57–63; DeConick, Original Gospel of Thomas in Translation (2006), 53–58. The note about this saying's already-presence in our Tier 3 section D (as Origen's GHeb citation) is preserved; the Thomas form is included here not as redundant duplication but as the Coptic transmission stream of the same saying.
  83. Logion 3 (Kingdom within). The Coptic ⲡⲉⲧⲛ̄ϩⲟⲩⲛ ⲛ̄ϩⲏⲧ ⲧⲏⲩⲧⲛ̄ is rendered "within you" — but the same Greek phrase entos hymōn lies behind Lk 17:21, where the rendering is contested between "within you" (interior-spiritual) and "among you" (in your midst, present-now). BDAG s.v. entos gives both as valid in Hellenistic Greek, with the spatial-among reading preferred for Lk 17:21 (so Marshall, Luke, NIGTC). The Thomasine formulation here pairs "within you" with "outside you," which suggests the saying intends both: the basileia is everywhere, both interior and exterior, and the recognition of this is what knowing-oneself entails. The "sons of the living Father" language is not exclusively gnostic — it parallels Mt 5:9, 5:45 ("sons of your Father in heaven"). The closing "you are the poverty" pulls toward Thomasine spiritual-poverty theology (cf. Logion 29) but is not body-suspicious as such. See Patterson, Gospel of Thomas (2013), 64–69; Davies, The Gospel of Thomas and Christian Wisdom (1983).
  84. Logion 6 (Don't lie, don't do what you hate). The negative Golden Rule ("what you hate, do not do") parallels the saying attributed to Hillel in b. Shabbat 31a ("what is hateful to you, do not do to your neighbor — that is the whole Torah") and the form preserved in Didache 1:2 and the Western text of Acts 15:20. This is solid evidence of the saying as part of pre-Christian Jewish ethical tradition that Jesus is plausibly drawing on or echoing. See Crossan, In Fragments (1983), 333–334; van de Sandt and Flusser, The Didache (2002), 162–169.
  85. Logion 11 (The heavens passing away). The saying is composite. The opening parallels Mk 13:31 // Mt 24:35 // Lk 21:33 ("heaven and earth shall pass away") and the multi-heavens cosmology is Jewish-apocalyptic (cf. 2 Cor 12:2 "third heaven"; 3 Enoch; Apocalypse of Abraham), not distinctively gnostic. The middle portion ("when you ate what is dead, you made it living") lacks clear parallel; some scholars connect it loosely to 1 Cor 15 (the dead being raised), but the connection is interpretive rather than textual. The closing question ("on the day when you were one, you became two; but when you have come to be two, what shall you do?") prefigures the "two becoming one" motif in Logion 22. Under the whole-logion principle, the saying is included as a unit; the apocalyptic opening's clear synoptic warrant carries the rest. See DeConick, The Original Gospel of Thomas (2005), 83–90; Patterson (2013), 75–79.
  86. Logion 14 (Fasting and what comes out of the mouth). The saying combines (a) a sharply anti-piety opening that pulls against the Mt 6:1–18 tradition of pious practice ("when you fast... when you pray... when you give alms"); (b) the mission discourse instruction (Lk 10:5–9 // Mt 10:11–13: eat what is set before you, heal the sick); (c) the defilement saying paralleling Mk 7:15. The anti-piety opening is hard. One reading: it is a wisdom-style hyperbole, structurally similar to the Cynic-style provocations Jesus elsewhere uses (Mk 2:18–22 "new wine in old wineskins"; Lk 16:8 "the children of this age are wiser"). Another reading: it reflects a Thomasine antinomian impulse, with the saying having been edited away from the canonical practice-of-piety formulation. The whole-logion principle carries the saying in. See Plisch, The Gospel of Thomas (2008), 67–69.
  87. Logion 16 (Discord, not peace). The saying parallels Q (Lk 12:51–53 // Mt 10:34–36) closely. Thomas adds the closing line "and they shall stand as monachos" — the first appearance of monachos in Thomas (also in 49 and 75). On monachos, see note on Logion 75 below. The "five in a house, three against two and two against three" enumeration is preserved in Thomas with one less generation than in Luke — Thomas omits Luke's "mother-in-law against daughter-in-law and daughter-in-law against mother-in-law." Klijn 1962 traces monachos through the Syriac ihidaya tradition and argues Thomas's use here is pre-monastic.
  88. Logion 17 (Eye has not seen). Paul cites this saying at 1 Cor 2:9 as scripture, prefacing it with "as it is written" (kathōs gegraptai). The source is contested — possibilities include Isaiah 64:4 (paraphrased), the Apocalypse of Elijah, an unknown apocryphon, or a free citation. The Thomas form is closer to Paul's citation than to any extant LXX text. This is suggestive evidence that Paul and Thomas share a sayings tradition that may predate both — possibly an early Jesus-saying treated as scripture in some early Christian communities. See Hurtado, Lord Jesus Christ (2003), 105; Stuhlmacher, Biblical Theology of the New Testament (2018), 257–259.
  89. Logion 21 (Children in the field; the thief; the strong-one). The saying is composite: (a) the cryptic children-in-the-field opening with no clean synoptic parallel; (b) the master-of-the-house-and-the-thief paralleling Q (Lk 12:39 // Mt 24:43 — also separately preserved in Logion 103); (c) gird-up-loins-against-bandits language; (d) the harvest-with-sickle parallel to Mk 4:29. The opening section reads as a parable of nakedness-before-the-world's-rulers, which has no canonical parallel but is not body-suspicious in the encratite sense — it is more a renunciation-of-property motif. Mariham (Mary) appears as the questioner; she figures in eight Thomas sayings (21, 114) including the controversial Logion 114 which is excluded from this canon. Her presence here as questioner does not in itself implicate the saying in the body-suspicious framework. See Patterson (2013), 88–95.
  90. Logion 22 (Infants nursing; making the two one). The saying preserves multiple distinct traditions: (a) the infants-being-suckled simile and the question about becoming infants — paralleling Mk 10:14–15 and Mt 18:3; (b) the catena of antithetical-into-one identifications (inside/outside, upper/lower, male/female); (c) the eye-in-place-of-eye, hand-in-place-of-hand, image-in-place-of-image catena. The "making the two one" formulation has direct canonical parallel in Eph 2:14–16 (Christ "having made the two one... that he might create the two in himself into one new human-being"); the male-and-female-into-one formulation has direct canonical parallel in Gal 3:28 ("there is no male and female; for you are all one in Christ Jesus") — note Paul's "no male and female" ouk eni arsen kai thēly deliberately quotes Genesis 1:27 LXX arsen kai thēly and inverts it. The same saying is also preserved in 2 Clement 12:2–6 (proto-orthodox 2nd-century work) attributed to "the Lord himself when asked by someone when his kingdom would come," and in Clement of Alexandria's citation of the Gospel of the Egyptians (Stromata 3.13.92) in similar form. The independent multiple attestation across canonical (Pauline, Ephesians), proto-orthodox (2 Clement), and apocryphal (Gospel of the Egyptians, Thomas) streams is unusually strong. The eye-in-place-of-eye / hand-in-place-of-hand catena reads as eschatological-restoration-of-the-image language with possible parallel to 1 Cor 15:35–49 (the body sown perishable, raised imperishable; the heavenly eikōn in place of the earthly). Eikōn is preserved untranslated to mark the Genesis 1:26–27 vocabulary (kat' eikona theou, "according to the image of God"). The saying as a whole reads as a Genesis-1 eschatology — the differentiations of creation reversed in the basileia. See MacDonald, There Is No Male and Female (1987); Meeks, "The Image of the Androgyne" History of Religions 13 (1974) 165–208. (The "androgyne" reading does pull toward Valentinian elaboration in later texts, but the underlying Pauline and 2 Clement parallels show the figure's roots are pre-Valentinian and within the canonical tradition's range.)
  91. Logion 24 (Light within). The saying parallels Q's lamp/light material (Lk 11:33–36 // Mt 5:14–16, "you are the light of the world"). The Coptic ⲣⲱⲙⲉ ⲛ̄ⲟⲩⲟⲉⲓⲛ — "human-being of light" — is preserved as phōteinos (the underlying Greek) with bracketed gloss "light-one." The "light within him gives light to all the kosmos" formulation has its closest canonical parallel in Mt 5:14–16 (the lamp-on-a-stand giving light to the whole house); the slight pull toward Thomasine interiority is in the "within" rather than the "set on a stand."
  92. Logion 29 (Spirit dwelling in flesh). The "great wealth dwelling in this poverty" framing has its closest parallel in 2 Cor 8:9, where Paul says the Anointed "being rich, for your sakes became poor, that you through his poverty might be rich." That is a Christological inversion of the same wealth-poverty figure. The dualistic body-spirit framing also parallels Wisdom of Solomon 9:15 ("a corruptible body weighs down the soul, and the earthy tent burdens the thoughtful mind"), which is canonical-deuterocanonical Jewish wisdom literature (the Septuagint includes Wisdom). The saying is not distinctively gnostic in formulation; the closest scholarly readings are (a) Patterson (2013): wisdom-tradition reflection on the paradox of incarnation; (b) DeConick (2005): Thomasine interior-spiritual reading. Sarx, pneuma, and sōma are preserved untranslated per the standing convention. See Patterson (2013), 102–105.
  93. Logion 39 (Keys of knowledge). Direct Q parallel to Lk 11:52 // Mt 23:13. The Greek loanword gnōsis is preserved untranslated; the underlying Greek of the saying probably had gnōseōs (genitive of gnōsis) where the canonical synoptics have gnōseōs (Lk 11:52) or basileias tōn ouranōn (Mt 23:13 — "kingdom of the heavens"). The Lukan "knowledge" form is closer to Thomas, suggesting Thomas preserves the Q reading where Matthew has redacted toward "kingdom." The "snakes and doves" addition at the end parallels Mt 10:16. The combination of these two synoptic-distinct sayings into one logion is Thomasine editorial work; both halves have canonical warrant.
  94. Logion 46 (From Adam to John). Direct Q parallel to Lk 7:28 // Mt 11:11. The Thomasine ending — "whoever among you shall become as a little-one shall know the basileia, and shall become higher than John" — diverges from the canonical "the least in the kingdom is greater than him." Where the canonical version compares positions (least in the kingdom > John), Thomas compares spiritual states (those who become as little-ones > John). The "becoming as little-one" connects to Mk 10:15 // Mt 18:3, so the divergence reads as a Thomasine merging of two synoptic sayings into one rather than as foreign theology. See Plisch (2008), 121.
  95. Logion 55 (Hate father and mother). Direct Q parallel to Lk 14:26–27 // Mt 10:37–38. Thomas's ⲛ̄ⲧⲁϩⲉ "in my way / as I do" is closer to Matthew's "more than me" than to Luke's stark "and not hate his own life also." See note on Logion 101 for the doubled form.
  96. Logion 74 (Many around the well). Distinctively Thomasine; included for proximity to Logion 73 (which has clear Q parallel, Lk 10:2). The "many at the trough but no one in the well" image reads as a wisdom-saying about superficial vs. deep engagement. The form is short enough that the Thomasine theological frame does not overwhelm; the image is general enough to admit non-gnostic reading. Whole-logion principle applies through proximity to 73. See Patterson (2013), 168.
  97. Logion 75 (The monachos and the bridal chamber). Monachos is preserved untranslated with bracketed gloss "single-ones / solitary-ones." The term is a Greek loanword in Coptic, appearing in Logia 16, 49, and 75. In the 2nd century, monachos was a more general term for "single," "solitary," "unique," "alone"; it later (4th century onward) became the technical term for "monk" in the Pachomian and Antonian monastic tradition. Klijn's classic 1962 study (Novum Testamentum 5) showed the term renders Syriac ihidaya — used both for celibates and for the unique-one (the Beloved Son, the Christ figure). In Thomas, the term carries the broader semantic range; rendering "monk" is anachronistic, and any single English equivalent ("solitary," "single one," "alone") loses range. The "many at the door" image parallels the synoptic narrow-gate / closed-door material at Lk 13:23–28 // Mt 7:13–14 and 25:10–12 ("Lord, Lord, open to us"). The "place of the wedding" / "bridal chamber" image connects to the synoptic wedding-feast and bridegroom material (Mt 22:1–14, Mt 25:1–13, Mk 2:18–20). The bridal-chamber later became a Valentinian sacrament (Gospel of Philip), but the Thomas usage probably predates that development and is metaphorical-eschatological rather than sacramental. See Klijn, "The 'Single One' in the Gospel of Thomas" JBL 81 (1962); Patterson (2013), 169–172; DeConick (2005), 215–217.
  98. Logion 79 (Blessed womb, blessed hearers). Direct parallel to Lk 11:27–28. Thomas adds the closing eschatological line "days shall come when you shall say: blessed is the womb that has not conceived, and the breasts that have not given milk" — which parallels Lk 23:29 (the way to the cross, "blessed are the barren"). Thomas thus combines two Lukan sayings into one. The combination has been read as anti-natalist in the encratite sense, but the Lukan parallel at 23:29 is set in eschatological-judgment context, not encratite — same here. See Plisch (2008), 184–186.
  99. Logion 90 (Easy yoke). Direct parallel to Mt 11:28–30 (Special M). The Coptic ⲭⲣⲏⲥⲧⲟⲥ — "kindly," "good," "easy" — is the Greek loanword chrēstos (preserved untranslated with bracketed gloss). The famous Greek wordplay between Christos (Anointed) and chrēstos (kindly) — pronounced identically in 2nd-century Greek — would not be visible in Coptic, but the underlying Greek surely had the punning resonance. See Hurtado (2003), 117 on the Christos/chrēstos phenomenon in early Christian sources.
  100. Logion 101 (Hate-and-love father and mother). The saying is structurally a doublet of Logion 55 (which has direct Q parallel at Lk 14:26–27 // Mt 10:37–38), but with the love-half added in antithetical parallelism. Three readings are possible: (a) the double form preserves a primitive aphoristic structure that the canonical hate-only version flattened (Crossan, In Fragments 1983, 121–129, on the double-stich form in Jewish wisdom literature); (b) the double form represents Thomasine softening, with the love-half added later to balance the harsh hate-half; (c) the two halves point to different referents — hate-the-earthly, love-the-spiritual — supported by the broken closing line ("for my mother [...]; but my true mother gave me zōē"). The lacuna at ⲧⲁⲙⲁⲁⲩ ⲅⲁⲣ ⲛ̄ⲧⲁⲥ[…] — "for my mother [...]" — is left unfilled here; the standard scholarly reconstructions are "[bore me]" or "[gave me a body]," contrasted with "but my true mother gave me zōē." The "true mother" language has an important parallel in the Gospel of the Hebrews: Origen (Commentary on John 2.12) and Jerome (Commentary on Micah 7.6, Commentary on Isaiah 40:9) both cite the Gospel of the Hebrews as having Jesus refer to "my mother, the Holy pneuma." This 2nd-century Jewish-Christian tradition of the Spirit as feminine spiritual mother is the most plausible context for the closing line. Zōē is preserved untranslated per the standing convention; the contrast is between being-given-a-body (the earthly mother's gift) and being-given-zōē (the spiritual mother's gift), structurally like the psychē / zōē contrast in John 12:25. See Crossan (1983), 121–129; Plisch (2008), 222–224; Klijn, Jewish-Christian Gospel Tradition (1992), 53–60. Reading (c) is most defensible given the textual evidence.
  101. Logion 107 (Lost sheep). Direct parallel to Q (Lk 15:4–7 // Mt 18:12–14). The Thomasine distinctive is the description of the sheep that wanders away as "the largest" (ⲡⲛⲟϭ) rather than simply "one" (Luke) or "one of them goes astray" (Matthew). The shepherd's closing word — "I love you more than the ninety-nine" — is also Thomasine; the canonical versions have rejoicing rather than declaration of preferential love. The divergence may reflect Thomas's interest in the one (the monachos, the singular soul) over the many — but it may equally reflect an independent tradition where the parable centered on the value of the wandering one rather than on the joy of recovery. The whole-logion principle includes the saying. See Plisch (2008), 233.
  102. P.Oxy 1224, general note. The two fragments (recto and verso of a single leaf) preserve six brief sequences. Only two have substantial parallel to canonical synoptic material — the sick-need-physician saying (Mk 2:17 // Mt 9:12 // Lk 5:31) and the for-or-against saying (Mk 9:40 // Lk 9:50). Crossan's analysis (in Miller, The Complete Gospels, 1992, 416–418) shows phrasing differences from the canonical versions sufficient to indicate textual independence rather than copying. The vision-saying ("Jesus drew near in a vision and said, 'Why are you of-faint-heart?'") is unparalleled in the canonical tradition and reads as either (a) a post-resurrection appearance fragment or (b) a saying about prayer-vision experience. The fragmentary state makes confident reading impossible. Editions: Grenfell & Hunt 1914; Kraus, Nicklas & Kruger 2009. See also Foster, "Papyrus Oxyrhynchus X 1224" in Kraus & Nicklas, Early Christian Manuscripts (2010), 59–96.
  103. P.Oxy 1224, fragment 2 recto col 1, "love your enemies" formulation. The opening line restoration "[and you] pray [...] for your enemies" plausibly parallels Q's love-of-enemies material (Lk 6:27–28, 35 // Mt 5:44 — already in our canon as Q-passage 4). The phrasing "the one not [against you] is [for you]" parallels Mk 9:40 ("the one not against us is for us") and Lk 9:50 — but with the second-person address rather than first-person plural, suggesting independent transmission. The "today / tomorrow" antithesis ("the one being far from you today, tomorrow shall be near to you") has no canonical parallel and reads as a wisdom-saying about the changeability of relations. See Foster (2010), 78–82.
  104. P.Oxy 1224, fragment 2 verso col 1, "new teaching" interrogation. The fragment reads as a controversy-dialogue: opponents asking Jesus what new teaching he proclaims, what new immersion he offers. The setting may be a Pharisee or scribal interrogation comparable to Mk 11:27–33 (the question of authority — already in our canon). The "new teaching" / "new immersion" formulation suggests early-Christian-baptismal context (cf. Acts 19:1–7 on rebaptism). See Foster (2010), 87–90.
  105. P.Oxy 1224, fragment 2 verso col 2, sick-need-physician saying. This is the most secure parallel to canonical material — Mk 2:17 // Mt 9:12 // Lk 5:31 ("the well need not a physician but the sick"). The setting (eating with the failed-ones, scribes-and-Pharisees-and-priests vexed) replicates the Mk 2:15–17 dining scene almost exactly, but with the addition of "and priests" alongside the scribes-and-Pharisees grouping. The "and priests" addition is unique to P.Oxy 1224 and suggests independent transmission rather than copying from any of the synoptics. See Foster (2010), 90–94; Crossan in Miller (1992), 417.
  106. P.Oxy 5575, general note. Published August 2023 by Fish, Wallace, and Holmes (The Oxyrhynchus Papyri LXXXVII, EES). The fragment dates to the late 2nd or early 3rd century, making it among the earliest extant Christian manuscripts and the earliest manuscript witness to material associated with the Gospel of Thomas (preceding the P.Oxy 1, 654, 655 fragments). The fragment's distinctive feature is that it interweaves material in a single discourse — moving between what we know in Matthew, Luke, and Thomas without clear seams. One editor (Holmes) suggests the fragment may be "a work which was not dependent upon Gos. Thom. but rather served as a source for it" (Text & Canon Institute, Sept 2023). If correct, this would make P.Oxy 5575 an unusually early witness to a sayings-source behind both the canonical Q-tradition and the Thomas tradition. The Matthean-parallel sections show Matthean redactional elements (textual dependence on canonical Matthew); the Thomas-parallel sections do not. Translated below are the non-Matthean-dependent sections. See also Coogan and Rodriguez 2024 (forthcoming critical analysis); Kruger, Canon Fodder blog 8 January 2024 on dependence questions.
  107. P.Oxy 5575, recto, rich-fool ending. The opening lines preserve the closing of a parable that closely parallels Thomas Logion 63 (the rich man planning his storehouses, who dies that night). The Thomas form is preserved only in Coptic; this fragment may be the earliest Greek witness to the parable's wording. The ending formula "the one having ears, let him hear" is the standard Thomasine closing for parables.
  108. P.Oxy 5575, "fast from the kosmos / keep the Sabbath". This pair parallels Thomas Logion 27: "If you do not fast from the kosmos, you shall not find the basileia; if you do not keep the Sabbath as Sabbath, you shall not see the Father." The Thomas form is preserved only in Coptic; this fragment is the earliest Greek witness. The "fasting from the kosmos" formulation is metaphorical — fasting-from-the-world rather than fasting-from-food — and reads as a wisdom-saying about renunciation comparable to the Pauline "use the world as not using it" (1 Cor 7:31). The Sabbath formulation may parallel the Sabbath material at Mk 2:23–28 // 3:1–6 (already in our canon).
  109. P.Oxy 5575, verso, "add a cubit to his stature". This formulation parallels Q's worry-saying at Lk 12:25 // Mt 6:27 ("which of you by being-anxious can add a pēchys to his hēlikia?") — the pēchys (cubit, ~18 inches) being either a measure of body-height (the standard reading) or of life-span (a less common but defensible reading, since hēlikia can mean "age" or "lifespan"). Pēchys is preserved untranslated with bracketed gloss "cubit" given the technical-measurement nature of the term. The "are you not better than them?" question reads as continuation of the birds-of-the-heaven material, suggesting this fragment preserved the worry-saying in something close to the Lukan form (where the cubit-and-stature line follows the birds line directly).
  110. Pericope Adulterae, text-critical status. The pericope is absent from the earliest and best manuscripts of John (P66, P75, Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) and from the Old Syriac, the Coptic Sahidic, and most early versions. Where it does appear in later manuscripts, it appears in widely varying locations — sometimes after Jn 7:52 (the standard place), sometimes after Jn 7:36, sometimes after Jn 21:25, and in some manuscripts after Lk 21:38 or Lk 24:53. The text-critical near-consensus is that the pericope is not original to John. NA28 and UBS5 mark it with double-square-brackets to signal its non-originality. However, the pericope is widely attested in Latin manuscripts from the 4th century onward and is referenced by Didymus the Blind (4th century) and possibly by Papias (Eusebius, HE 3.39.17, mentions Papias having a story about a woman accused of many sins before the Lord). Metzger's Textual Commentary concludes: "the account has all the earmarks of historical veracity. It is obviously a piece of oral tradition which circulated in certain parts of the Western church." Its inclusion in this canon is on the same basis as Lk 23:34a — a "floating dominical tradition" with strong narrative coherence with the synoptic Jesus, included with explicit text-critical marking rather than silent inclusion. See Metzger, Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament (1994), 187–189; Ehrman, The Orthodox Corruption of Scripture (1993), 165–167; Keith, The Pericope Adulterae (2009).
  111. Pericope Adulterae, "writing into the earth" and "the without-hamartia one". The detail of Jesus stooping and writing on the ground (V 6, repeated V 8) is one of the most discussed elements in the pericope. Patristic suggestions include: (a) Jesus writing the failures of the accusers in the dust (the Jeremiah 17:13 reading: "those who turn away from you shall be written in the earth"); (b) Jesus performing the gesture of refusal-to-engage-with-the-trap; (c) Jesus inscribing the letter of the Mosaic law to demonstrate its true demand. None of these can be confirmed; the pericope itself does not say what was written. The "the without-hamartia one of you, first upon her let-him-cast a stone" (V 7) inverts the legal-witness procedure of Deut 17:7 (the witnesses' hands are to be first against the condemned) — Jesus does not deny the law but raises its evidentiary standard. Hamartia is preserved untranslated per the standing convention. See Keith (2009); Hill, The Lost Gospel of the Pericope Adulterae (2018).
